Chapter One

The Quiet
Pantry

The Japanese pantry begins with absence. Walk into a well-kept home kitchen in Tokyo and you may be struck not by what is there, but by what is not — no spice rack crowding the counter, no dozen oils. A few jars on a low shelf, a wooden bowl, the morning light.

This is the first lesson of Japanese home cooking, and the most difficult one for cooks trained in other traditions: fewer ingredients, used well, will always taste better than many ingredients used poorly. What follows is a list of what I keep — and, by absence, what I do not.

What lives on the shelf

Short-grain rice, never more than ten days from its bag opening. Kombu — Rishiri if I can find it, Rausu otherwise — wrapped tightly in paper, never plastic. Katsuobushi in a sealed jar, away from light. Three soy sauces: usukuchi for clear soups, koikuchi for everything else, and a small bottle of tamari for the rare evening when a piece of fish wants depth.

What does not

I do not keep dried herbs. I do not keep more than one vinegar at a time — when one is gone I buy the next, and learn it slowly. I do not keep bouillon cubes; their job is done better by twenty minutes of patience and a square of kombu.

Chapter Two

Rice,
Three Ways

If dashi is the foundation of the meal, rice is its architecture. In a Japanese home, the rice is what makes the meal a meal — without it, you are simply eating dishes that happen to be next to each other on a table.

Film 02 · 03:12

Washing and steaming short-grain rice

Gohan · the everyday

Wash the rice in three changes of water, then a fourth that comes clear. Let it rest in cold water for thirty minutes — no longer, no shorter. One cup of rice to one and one-tenth cups of water; bring to a boil, drop to the lowest flame, and listen. When the sound of the water disappears, the rice is done.

Recipe · 40 minutes · Serves 4

The everyday rice

Sumeshi · for the special evening

Sushi rice is gohan, fanned to a sheen, then turned three times with a vinegar mixture — rice vinegar, sugar, salt — that has been warmed just enough to dissolve but never to boil. The fan is not a performance; it is what gives the rice its quiet shine.

Chapter Three

Dashi
三 · 出汁

There is a moment, just before the kombu releases its colour, when the water in the pot turns from clear to nearly clear — a shift you see only if you are paying attention. This is the beginning of every Japanese meal I have ever made.

Film 03 · 04:18

Drawing dashi from cold water and time

Notes on the kombu

Use Rishiri or Rausu if you can find it. Wipe — never wash — the kombu with a dry cloth. The white powder is not dust; it is mannitol, and it is the flavour you are after. A 10cm square of kombu in one litre of cold water, for one hour, then a slow rise to just before boiling.

Recipe · 25 minutes · Serves 4

The basic dashi

On the second pull

The kombu and the bonito have given you a clear, fragrant first broth — ichiban-dashi, for clear soups and the lightest things. But there is a second broth, niban-dashi, drawn from the same ingredients with more time and a touch more water. This is the broth for everyday cooking.

Chapter Four

Vegetables,
off-season

It is easy to cook well with the vegetables of the spring market — the first peas, the bamboo shoots, the soft greens. The harder task, the one that separates a cook from a careful one, is what to make of a turnip in late February.

The answer, almost always, is to do less. A vegetable past its season is not a vegetable to be hidden under sauces. It is a vegetable to be cut more thinly, salted earlier, and met with one other thing — a piece of yuzu peel, a drop of sesame oil, the slightest miso glaze.

The slow turnip

Quarter the turnips; salt them and wait twenty minutes. What comes off them is the water of the cold months, and what remains is sweeter than you would have guessed. Glaze them in a quiet pan with mirin and a small spoon of white miso. Finish with a thread of the dashi from Chapter Three.

Chapter Five

Fish, on a
Tuesday evening

Mackerel is the fish I have eaten more than any other. It is the fish of weekday evenings — quick, oily enough to forgive a hot grill, cheap enough to make often. There is no dignity lost in calling it ordinary; ordinary is what we cook the most.

Salt, then patience

Lay the mackerel skin-down on a plate. Salt it more than you think — a soft rain of salt, on both sides — and let it rest for thirty minutes. Water will come up. Pat it dry. This is the only preparation that matters; the rest is heat, and time, and watching.

Film 14 · 03:42

Salt-grilling a whole mackerel

Recipe · 25 minutes · Serves 2

Salt-grilled mackerel

What goes with it

A bowl of rice, a small pile of grated daikon, a wedge of sudachi or lemon. Soy sauce only if the fish was older than today's market. This is a meal of fifteen minutes from the moment the grill is hot, and it is the meal I cook most often, in any kitchen, in any city.

Chapter Six

The bowl that
closes the day

The last bowl of a Japanese evening is the smallest one. It comes after the rice, after the fish, after the small dishes — when the table is already half-cleared, and the kettle has come to its second boil of the night.

Ochazuke is hot tea, or sometimes dashi, poured over what is left of the rice in the bowl. With it goes a piece of pickled plum, a square of nori, perhaps a small mound of salted salmon flaked from the morning's grill. It is a meal that costs nothing and takes a minute, but it is the one I would choose, if I had to choose one meal to keep.

Recipe · 5 minutes · Serves 1

Ochazuke · お茶漬け

An ending

A cookbook is a letter to someone you have not yet met — to the person who, one evening, will open a window and let the steam from a small bowl rise into a kitchen I will never see. I have tried, in these pages, to write the letter I would have wanted to receive. Thank you for opening it.

— Yuki Tanaka
